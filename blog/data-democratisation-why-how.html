Data democratisation: How and Why?

Tech circles have been marred by endless debate about who owns data and who says what to do with it. Big Tech, a term used to refer to the four (or is it five?) dominant tech companies, has been accused of using user data at their disposal to create unfair business advantage. It’s an emotional topic because users do not even know, neither do most of them have the ability to know, how their personal information is used to predict and even determine their behaviour. Is there a problem? And, what’s the solution?

Funny strange, no one can pinpoint the exact problem, hence, we cannot have an exact solution.

Data democratisation refers to making data accessible to many people. Seems like it means making many people access the same data we are furious about. It’s a wide topic really. But today we’ll look at issues around data democratisation … and use cases.

However, the introduction above deviates us from our sphere of influence. Data democratisation, as a term in tech circles, has been used widely within the organisation context. Therefore, it refers to how everyone in an organisation has enough data – or can access data - at their disposal to enable them to make sound decisions as their role in the organisation demands. For example, salesmen may need to know the demography of clients who take up the organisation’s product faster than the others. With this information, they can narrow their target and spend fewer work hours to achieve their sales targets.

Data is changing fast. That presents challenges every day. It has become a necessity to have data teams in the organisation. It’s not even debatable. However, data teams are having hurdles too;


Hurdles faced in data democratisation

The analytics tools are not flexible; hence departments have to get the results that the data teams perceive they need.

Workers do not all know how to use the data they have.

Due to culture, users may not have confidence in the data and prefer to use traditional methods to accomplish their tasks.

Not all teams can access the data they need – mostly due to hierarchical authorisations to databases.

Data teams may not be accessible to everyone when needed.


Solutions to these problems;

Educate staff on; what type of data is essential for the firm, how to identify trends in the data needed and how to use the data available to make decisions.

Data teams need to work on data models that are understandable and relatable to departments that are not data-skilled.

Data visualisation; data teams need to work on the data and visualise it using the available visualisation tools to make it digestible to other departments. Good organisation data policy can demand this to be implemented periodically so that departments know when to expect the data they need to make the decisions they have to make.

Data teams can structure databases to allow access to everyone who needs data without compromising data security. The data architecture refers to the whole infrastructure that demonstrates how data is acquired, stored, queried, secured and disposed of.


Meanwhile, there is a lot of consideration that should go into data democratisation. For instance, client data in the CRM may be used by product departments predictively while the same data is used by marketing teams to identify trends to use for real-time call-to-action. Therefore, data democratisation starts with the question; who can use what data?
